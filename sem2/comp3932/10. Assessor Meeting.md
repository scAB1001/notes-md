### Neat
Academic projects should provide evidence of a much  
deeper understanding of what you are doing. They require  
justification and insights. You are not expected to merely do  
what you are told to do; you are expected to develop and  
explain thoughts, arguments, ideas, and concepts. You are  
expected to question things and look at things in new ways  
and from new angles.  

Merely doing what you are told does not lead to intellectual  
discovery or contributions to knowledge. As a degree  
student you are expected to think, and to demonstrate your  
thinking in a written report. This ‘deeper’ understanding of  
situations, problems, and events is supported by your  
research skills. Demonstrating your ability to undertake an  
academic project is a critical component of your degree  
course

Dawson outlines five features of computing projects. These are not  
mutually exclusive; your project should include three or more categories.  
The nature of your project will influence the methods you use to tackle it.  
- Review-based: systematic review of solutions to related tasks  
- Development: software / system development  
- Evaluation: comparing alternative solutions to a task  
- User-based: solving a requirement for a “client/user”  
- Problem Solving: a new technique to solve a known problem

#### Additional Considerations
The ‘so what?’ test
- Ask yourself, ‘Is the topic meaningful?’ will it be of value to anybody? What contributions will it make?  
- Pursuing a meaningless project can lead to poor motivation as your project progresses.  

Explainability  
- Can you explain your project and justify it in simple terms to an average person? If so, you have a good understanding of the subject area and the topic you want to pursue.  

Contacts  
- When you identify the contacts you require for your project, are they available, accessible and willing to help? Eg users for requirement specification, evaluation

Understanding what you need to know  
- Do you have enough knowledge about your chosen subject? You must be aware of limits in your own understanding of the topic  
- For example, can you specify what you need to know about your chosen subject?  
- What new skills do you need to develop, new programming languages or new techniques you need to learn?

The pages preceding the

preliminaries, appendices, and references, but includes figures and tables (except those in appendices).

Appendices: Two appendices are compulsory. Appendix A must include all of the material to be assessed under the Self-appraisal criterion in the mark scheme.

#### Further writing tips  
a) Style of language  
Your reports are technical documents, and like all technical documents should be  
written in precise, impersonal language. Do not use subjective language (`I did  
this...', etc.), except possibly for those aspects that are covered by the 'self-  
appraisal' criterion in the mark scheme. Using 'we' is, however, acceptable. For  
guidance, look at the articles published in academic journals that you have  
probably already read as part of your background research.  

b) Version control of your written documents  
To make sure you know which document is the most recent and can trace back to  
earlier version to retrieve deleted content if needed. It can be as simple as adding  
v1, v2 to the file name.  

c) Referencing written work and use of a reference management system  
Our School accepts both Harvard and Numeric style of referencing. Once you  
have chosen the style, it needs to be consistently applied throughout the report.  
There are tools that make life easier for you to manage those references:  
Endnote https://endnote.com/ and Mendeley https://www.mendeley.com

#### e) Aiming high  
A well written and interesting report is likely to gain more marks than one that just follows the marking scheme (i.e. just ticking the boxes); in particular it will make the difference between a good project that is awarded over 70, and an excellent project that wins a prize. A well written report flows nicely, is presented logically, and is easy to understand. More time should be spent describing the challenging parts, and less time spent describing routine, or obvious, actions (though they often do need to be present). Reports that have the potential to attract a prize display a "spark" which is difficult to describe, but easy to detect; in particular it is clear to the reader that the author has fully mastered the subject.
