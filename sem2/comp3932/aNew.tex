\section{Critical Self-Evaluation}
\label{sec:self_eval}
% =============================================================================

Overall, I consider this project a success.
Without any prior experience in the hardware, software or experimental
methodology it demanded, I built a sophisticated system capable of
assessing the viability of bare-hand gesture interaction under
scientific standards, as a proposed solution to the cognitive
dissonance gap in surgical planning.
There are, however, many things I would approach differently with the
benefit of hindsight.

\textbf{Project selection.}
Even before the project began, I made decisions I would reconsider.
Supervisor selection took place at the very start of the academic term
under considerable time pressure, and my shortlist covered entirely
unrelated fields simply because individual project ideas appealed to
me in isolation.
The domain of this project spans computer graphics, medical imagery
and human-computer interaction, none of which I had meaningful
experience in.
I do not regret the choice --- the breadth proved formative --- but
I would now advise any student to at least ensure their chosen project
sits within a domain they have some footing in before committing.

\textbf{Hardware access and planning.}
The most consequential change I would make is gaining lab access
immediately rather than waiting until mid-February, approximately
12 weeks into a 20-week project window.
Bureaucratic delays --- including requiring supervisor administrative
credentials to install any software --- were irritating but manageable.
The real problem was beginning hardware integration with no prior
experience of the Varjo XR-4 or Ultraleap LMC2.
At upwards of \pounds5,000 for the headset alone, I naively expected
reliable out-of-the-box operation.
Both devices were subject to intermittent cable, software, overheating
and unexplained faults whose only resolution was often to wait, restart
all services, or accept partial data.
Were I to advise someone entering a similarly hardware-dependent project,
I would tell them to stress-test the full device stack within the first
week and, if it proves genuinely unusable, communicate this to their
supervisor and seek alternative hardware before architectural decisions
are made around it.

\textbf{Literature review.}
I am proud of the systematic approach I took once I began the literature
review: collating sources, building a profile for each that captured key
information at a glance, grouping by theme and ranking by relevance.
This proved invaluable during writing, as I had already digested and
annotated each source rather than returning to them cold.
The regret is that I started too late.
Competing assignments pushed the literature review aside, and I
ultimately compressed a body of genuinely complex material into
approximately one week.
That pace was insufficient to fully internalise the theoretical
depth this project required --- particularly the embodiment, fatigue and
statistical literature --- and I retained less than I should have.
The profile system partially compensated for this, but the lesson is
clear: literature review should begin in parallel with project selection,
not after it.

\textbf{Version control and engineering practice.}
My use of Git and the GitHub platform is something I am genuinely proud
of.
Rule sets, branch protection, Conventional Commits, milestone-linked
issue templates, labelled sub-issues, Kanban and Roadmap board views,
CI Actions and Semantic Versioning releases shaped my development
workflow end-to-end.
These are not decorative features; they meant I could always locate
context for any decision, roll back safely and hand off the codebase
in an auditable state.
I note that Git is not standard practice in Unity projects, where
binary asset sizes often make repository management difficult.
For this project it was appropriate, but any expansion introducing
multiple actors, large scene files or binary assets at scale would
benefit from a more specialised version control strategy, such as
Git LFS with Unity Plastic SCM integration.

The DFSM architecture was the single best design decision of the
project.
Enforcing totality across all 36 input-state combinations and routing
both modalities through an abstract provider contract eliminated an
entire class of undefined runtime behaviour that had plagued the
prototype.
This was a full-circle moment: I had performed poorly on the Formal
Languages and Finite Automata module and now the theory I struggled
with became the structural core of the production system.

\textbf{Sprint timeline and personal cost.}
My milestones operated on two-week sprints which I technically met,
but doing so required unreasonable working hours and personal
sacrifices that a better-distributed workload would have avoided.
This was partly a knock-on effect of delayed lab access compressing
the implementation window, but the sprint durations themselves were
also too ambitious for a project of this scope.
In future I would plan for three-week sprints with a defined slack
day per sprint, and I would refuse to treat sprint overrun as
a personal failure rather than a planning signal.

\textbf{Experimental outcomes.}
The CONSORT-compliant trial protocol was well-executed: the condition
order justification, exclusion criteria and statistical analysis plan
were all pre-registered in the methodology rather than retrofitted to
results.
The trials themselves ran more smoothly than expected given my lack of
prior experience conducting human studies.
Unfortunately, the Varjo XR-4 continued to fail intermittently during
live sessions; on some occasions a full trial could not be completed in
a single sitting, which likely introduced minor temporal confounds in
the affected participants' data.
Three of eight enrolled participants were ultimately excluded due to
headset subsystem faults producing malformed telemetry exports, reducing
the analysed sample to $n = 5$ against the $n \geq 8$ minimum I had
planned for.
This meant the Wilcoxon Signed-Rank test could not produce $p < .05$
for any two-tailed comparison regardless of effect magnitude.
I adapted by foregrounding effect sizes as the primary evidential
metric, following the dual-reporting approach of
\citet{Rantamaa2023ComparisonReality}, and reframing the study
explicitly as a pilot feasibility trial rather than a confirmatory one.
Had I budgeted one or two contingency enrolment sessions from the
outset, the final sample would likely have been adequate.

\section{Personal Reflection and Lessons Learned}
\label{sec:reflection}
% =============================================================================

\textbf{Technical growth.}
Before this project I had not worked with quaternion mathematics in a
production context, had not implemented a formal automaton in software
and had not conducted a within-subjects experimental study.
I leave the project confident in all three.
The most intellectually satisfying moment was deriving the formal proof
that the DFSM accepts exactly the language of valid interaction
sequences, connecting the theoretical computer science I had previously
struggled with to the physical interaction behaviour of the live trials
in a way that felt genuinely earned.

\textbf{Project management.}
The Kanban work-in-progress limit of three concurrent issues forced
the kind of prioritisation decisions I would otherwise have deferred
indefinitely.
The clearest lesson, however, is that hardware-dependent projects
require an explicit risk buffer: I spent approximately 20\% of
Milestone~2 resolving HDRP and device integration issues that I had
not budgeted for, compressing the gesture recognition development
window.
In any future hardware-dependent project, I would schedule a dedicated
device validation sprint before the first implementation sprint,
treating integration as a first-class deliverable rather than a
background assumption.
I would also extend sprint durations from two to three weeks and
build slack into each one rather than treating overrun as a personal
shortcoming.

\textbf{Research methodology.}
Running a human study revealed a gap between planning and execution that
no amount of pre-registration fully closes.
I had designed the statistical analysis plan carefully but had not
stress-tested the trial protocol with a single informal pilot
participant before the main cohort.
A dry-run session would have surfaced the headset weight discomfort
reported by VR-naïve participants and the intermittent device faults
that caused session interruptions, allowing me to add an acclimatisation
period and a formal equipment checklist to the protocol.
Both of these are now permanent fixtures in any experimental checklist
I will produce.

\textbf{Communication and writing.}
Writing this dissertation required me to maintain three distinct
registers simultaneously: non-specialist for the Introduction, methodologist
for the statistics sections and software engineer for the implementation.
The discipline of holding those registers separately --- not
front-loading technical acronyms, not assuming familiarity with
either experimental design or automata theory --- is a skill I had
not consciously developed before.
The same challenge applies to the self-appraisal itself: moving from
the impersonal scientific voice of the main text to first-person
reflection required a conscious shift that I found more difficult than
expected.
Both transitions are ones I will navigate more confidently in future
technical writing.

\textbf{Lessons summarised.}
The three most transferable lessons from this project are: begin
hardware integration and literature review in the first week of the
project, not the first month; plan experimental contingency capacity
explicitly rather than assuming nominal conditions; and treat sprint
overrun as a planning signal requiring scope reduction, not a
motivation to work longer hours.
