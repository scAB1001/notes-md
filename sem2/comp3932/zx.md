A within-subjects pilot crossover study was conducted with eight enrolled participants ($n = 5$ analysed after equipment-failure exclusions). Each participant completed the full four-task sequence under both CTR and HTR conditions. Objective telemetry --- Task Completion Time (TCT), positional and rotational error, path inefficiency, clutch count and grip variance --- was supplemented by the Raw NASA Task Load Index (RTLX), administered post-task, and the System Usability Scale (SUS), administered post-condition.

CTR achieved substantially higher usability than HTR ($M = 87.0$, $SD = 9.9$ vs $M = 67.5$, $SD = 15.5$; $W = 1.0$, $p = .125$, $d = -1.050$), placing it in the Excellent band against HTR's Okay--Fair boundary. Kinematic telemetry revealed consistent and large performance deficits under HTR, most critically at the 6-DoF combined task where TCT was 3.1-fold longer ($d = 2.671$) and final rotational error was 5.3-fold higher ($d = 1.781$), both approaching the minimum achievable Wilcoxon statistic ($W = 0.0$, $p = .063$). HTR clutch counts exceeded CTR by 8--15-fold across three tasks, directly indexing the delimitation problem and Heisenberg effect, where the actuation of a pinch gesture inadvertently displaces the virtual target. RTLX Frustration showed the strongest workload trend, with HTR producing nearly double the frustration of CTR ($M = 6.85$ vs $M = 3.60$; $W = 0.0$, $p = .125$).

The findings demonstrate that while HTR offers superior psychological embodiment and is strongly preferred for near-field tasks in principle, optical occlusion, gesture instability and the Heisenberg effect remain decisive barriers to clinical adoption at this hardware generation. CTR's haptic grounding and deterministic input continue to outperform bare-hand tracking across all primary outcome measures for sustained, high-density 6-DoF spatial manipulation. The study establishes the first empirical baseline for CTR versus HTR on Varjo XR-4 and LMC2 hardware, providing the effect size parameters required to power a confirmatory trial and identifying gaze-assisted multi-modal interaction as the most promising architectural direction for resolving the remaining spatial bottlenecks.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Prototype kinematic manipulator (monolithic, Ultraleap-specific).}
\label{alg:proto_kinematic_manipulator}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Statex \textbf{State:} \texttt{isGrabbing} $\gets$ \textbf{false};\enspace \texttt{activeHandId} $\gets -1$;\enspace \texttt{positionOffset};\enspace \texttt{rotationOffset}
\Statex

\Procedure{Update}{}
    \If{\texttt{leapProvider.CurrentFrame} $=$ \textbf{null}} \Return \EndIf
    \If{$\neg$\texttt{isGrabbing}}
            \State \Call{TryBeginGrab}{\texttt{CurrentFrame}}
            \Else
        \State \Call{UpdateGrab}{\texttt{CurrentFrame}}
                \EndIf
\EndProcedure

\Statex

\Procedure{TryBeginGrab}{\texttt{frame}}
    \For{each \texttt{hand} $\in$ \texttt{frame.Hands}}
        \If{\texttt{hand.PinchDistance} $\leq$ \texttt{grabThreshold}}
                \State \texttt{activeHandId} $\gets$ \texttt{hand.Id}
                    \State \texttt{isGrabbing} $\gets$ \textbf{true}
                    \State \texttt{positionOffset} $\gets$ \texttt{targetMesh.position} $-$ \Call{PinchCenter}{\texttt{hand}}
                    \State \texttt{rotationOffset} $\gets$ $\texttt{hand.Rotation}^{-1} \otimes \texttt{targetMesh.rotation}$
                                \State \textbf{break}
                \EndIf
    \EndFor
\EndProcedure

\Statex

\Procedure{UpdateGrab}{\texttt{frame}}
    \State \texttt{hand} $\gets$ \texttt{frame.Hand(activeHandId)}
    \If{\texttt{hand} $=$ \textbf{null} \textbf{or} \texttt{hand.PinchDistance} $\geq$ \texttt{releaseThreshold}}
        \State \texttt{isGrabbing} $\gets$ \textbf{false};\enspace \texttt{activeHandId} $\gets -1$
                \State \Return
    \EndIf
    \If{\texttt{allowTranslation}}
            \State \texttt{targetMesh.position} $\gets$ \Call{PinchCenter}{\texttt{hand}} $+$ \texttt{positionOffset}
            \EndIf
    \If{\texttt{allowRotation}}
            \State \texttt{targetMesh.rotation} $\gets$ \texttt{hand.Rotation} $\otimes$ \texttt{rotationOffset}
            \EndIf
\EndProcedure

\Statex

\Procedure{PinchCenter}{\texttt{hand}}
    \State \texttt{thumb} $\gets$ \texttt{hand.fingers[THUMB].TipPosition}
    \State \texttt{index} $\gets$ \texttt{hand.fingers[INDEX].TipPosition}
    \State \Return $\texttt{Lerp}(\texttt{thumb},\ \texttt{index},\ 0.5)$
        \EndProcedure

\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

% ALGORITHM: WELFORD'S VARIANCE
% % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % %
\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Welford's online variance algorithm.}
\label{alg:welford_procedural}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Procedure{Variance}{samples}
    \State $M \gets 0$; \quad $S \gets 0$; \quad $k \gets 0$
    \For{each $x$ in samples}
        \State $k \gets k + 1$
                \State $\mathit{oldM} \gets M$
                \State $M \gets M + (x - M) \;/\; k$
                \State $S \gets S + (x - M) \times (x - \mathit{oldM})$
                \EndFor
    \State \Return $S \;/\; (k - 1)$
            \EndProcedure
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\chapter{Introduction and Background Research}

% =============================================================================
\section{Context}
\label{sec:ctx}
% =============================================================================

Liver cancer is a leading cause of cancer death worldwide \citep{Bray2018GlobalCountries, BrayBsc2024GlobalCountries}. Surgical resection is often the only curative option, but the liver's complex vasculature makes planning error‑prone: mistaking a vessel can cause incomplete tumour removal or post‑operative failure \citep{Fan2002ResectionAspects}.

Historically, surgeons planned by studying 2D images of patient anatomy, mentally reconstructing the full 3D shape. This was complex and cognitively exhausting. Modern software converts those images into interactive 3D models, which dramatically improves spatial understanding and reduces planning errors \citep{Kenngott2021IMHOTEP:Display}. However, these input methods have not kept pace with the models themselves.

Most VR surgical systems still use traditional physical handheld controllers, referred to throughout as controller tracking (CTR). To manipulate a 3D liver, a surgeon must perform unnatural actions: press buttons and push joysticks. The brain must translate the desired action into abstract controller input, reintroducing the cognitive effort that 3D models were designed to eliminate \citep{Caggianese2019TheEvaluation, Allgaier2022ATraining}.

An alternative is camera‑based hand tracking (HTR), which creates a direct mapping between hand and model movement However, cameras can lose tracking when fingers overlap; without a physical object, fine movements are harder to stabilise and sustained mid‑air use causes arm fatigue faster than holding a controller \citep{Hincapie-Ramos2014ConsumedInteractions}. Whether HTR genuinely outperforms CTR for precise, sustained surgical planning is an open question.
% =============================================================================
\section{The Proposed Solution}
\label{sec:nui_and_htr}
% =============================================================================

This project directly compares HTR and CTR using a VR liver alignment task. Participants moved, rotated and resized a 3D liver model (referred to throughout as the liver mesh or target mesh) to match a transparent target replica, replicating the spatial demands of resection planning. The task was decomposed into four stages of increasing difficulty, progressing from simple positional movement through combined movement and rotation to full manipulation including size adjustment. This structure allowed the comparison to identify not only which approach performs better overall but at which specific level of spatial complexity the two modalities diverge.

Performance was measured both objectively (task duration, alignment accuracy and movement efficiency) and subjectively (perceived mental effort per task and overall system usability), providing a complete picture of where each modality succeeds and where it falls short.
\chapter{Literature Review}
\label{chap2:lit_review}
% =============================================================================
\section{Clinical Applications of Medical VR}
\label{sec:clinical_applications}
% =============================================================================

Mentally reconstructing a patient's liver, its tumours and blood vessels from 2D CT and MRI slices is cognitively exhausting and error-prone, depending heavily on the surgeon's individual spatial ability \citep{Shi2025TranslatingEvaluation}. Errors in this reconstruction can lead to incomplete tumour resection, vascular damage or postoperative liver failure \citep{Fan2002ResectionAspects}. While 3D printing offers an alternative, it remains prohibitively costly and time-consuming for routine clinical use \citep{Lau2022ClinicalDisease, Ponzoni2025MixedDefects}. Clinicians have therefore adopted 3D computer-assisted surgery planning (CASP) tools that render volumetric models from imaging data \citep{Radtke2010Computer-assistedPeriod}. Until recently, however, these models were displayed on conventional 2D screens, which lack depth perception and motion parallax --- cues that surgeons rely on to interpret spatial relationships confidently. This dimensional mismatch is compounded by the shift towards minimally invasive surgery (MIS), where surgeons lose further spatial information. \citet{Sadeghi2020ImmersiveDisease} argue that VR support is becoming mandatory for modern surgical practice.
\citet{Kenngott2021IMHOTEP:Display} validated that immersive 3D visualisation bridges the cognitive gap caused by 2D scans across all levels of medical expertise. Beyond single-user experiences, \citet{Chheang2021APlanning} demonstrated collaborative, networked VR environments for liver tumour planning, where multidisciplinary teams debate resection trajectories via tracked avatars. Collectively, the literature establishes that manipulating a 3D liver model --- translating, rotating and scaling it to align with a target hologram --- is an optimal stimulus for replicating the spatial demands of resection planning. This polygonal liver model is referred to throughout as the target mesh, liver model or volumetric data. However, a fundamental gap persists: while the visual benefits of VR are well-documented, the input modality driving these interactions has not been thoroughly evaluated. Specifically, standard physical VR controllers have not been empirically compared against the latest bare-hand optical tracking technology in this application. The proposed system aims to address this gap directly through a controlled, single-user liver docking task, referred throughout as the proposed simulation or alignment task.

\section{Theoretical Foundations of 3D Human-Computer Interaction}
\label{sec:3d_interaction_hci_tax}
% =============================================================================
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Historical Evolution of 3D Interfaces}
\label{sec:historical_3d}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The pursuit of intuitive spatial computing predates modern optical sensors. Early Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) research explored 3D mice and tethered magnetic trackers to bridge physical movement and digital control \citep{Bowman2001TestbedTechniques, Soukoreff2004TowardsHCI}. These systems exposed the theoretical benefits of 3D spatial input but suffered significant practical failures: wired devices restricted user movement, while magnetic trackers offered small operational ranges and were susceptible to metallic interference, rendering them unsuitable for precision-critical medical applications \citep{Argelaguet2013AEnvironments}. This trajectory underscores a continuous drive towards frictionless interaction, where technology vanishes and natural movements translate directly into digital commands. Systems simply must develop beyond the archaic Windows, Icons, Menus, Pointer (WIMP) paradigm.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Transition to Natural User Interaction}
\label{sec:wimp_to_nui}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Navigating volumetric data using 2D peripherals introduces severe cognitive friction \citep{Bachmann2018ReviewController}. The user must translate intended 3D actions into 2D mouse movements or joystick deflections, buffering the interaction process. Consequently, HCI has pivoted towards Natural User Interfaces (NUI), which leverage innate human motor skills to eliminate this intermediary mapping step. Within this domain, \citet{Bachmann2018ReviewController} establish the Leap Motion Controller (LMC) as the academic standard for marker-less optical hand tracking, where markers refer to physical attachments placed on the body and optical tracking refers to the sensor recording visual data. These systems achieve a one-to-one (1:1) correspondence between physical hand movement and digital action, forgoing the abstract mapping required by physical controllers. The two modalities evaluated in this project are referred to throughout as Controller Tracking (CTR) and Hand Tracking (HTR).
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Degrees of Freedom in Spatial Interaction}
\label{sec:dof}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Central to evaluating any spatial interaction system is the concept of Degrees of Freedom (DoF): the number of independent axes along which a rigid body can move or rotate. In three-dimensional space, a rigid body has six independent degrees of freedom --- three translational, corresponding to movement along the $X$, $Y$ and $Z$ axes and three rotational, corresponding to pitch, yaw and roll \citep{Ackerman2007PitchTraits}. Full 6-DoF control is therefore required to position and orient an object arbitrarily in 3D space \citep{Bowman2001TestbedTechniques}.

For the proposed system, an additional dimension of uniform scaling is required to match the size of the liver model to the target hologram. This combination of translation, rotation and scale constitutes the complete manipulation space of the docking task. Decomposing the task into isolated DoF subsets --- pure translation, pure rotation, combined 6-DoF and full manipulation with scaling --- enables modality differences to be attributed to specific kinematic demands rather than to general task difficulty. This decomposition strategy is formalised by the Testbed Evaluation framework described in Section~\ref{sec:testbed_evaluation}.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Core Interaction Metaphors}
\label{sec:core_metaphors}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

To systematically evaluate user performance across these DoF subsets, spatial tasks must be classified within a formal HCI taxonomy. \citet{LI2019GestureReality} categorise virtual interaction into three foundational metaphors: Selection (target acquisition), Manipulation (translation and rotation) and Navigation (locomotion). In surgical VR, translating raw human motion into accurate digital intent is paramount. While the taxonomy is widely adopted, it collapses continuous 6-DoF manipulation into a single category, potentially obscuring differences between translational and rotational performance. The proposed system measures Selection and Manipulation both in isolation and in combination, ensuring evaluation as specific, quantifiable kinematic tasks rather than a generic interaction experience.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Testbed Evaluation Framework}
\label{sec:testbed_evaluation}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Evaluating spatial interfaces in application-specific environments is difficult because confounding variables increase with task complexity, threatening internal validity as meaning cannot be derived when uncontrolled factors have significant influence. \citep{Bowman2001TestbedTechniques}. The Testbed Evaluation framework mandates decomposition of complex procedures into distraction-free, isolated and highly controlled sub-tasks, enabling strict manipulation of the independent variable while capturing dependent metrics reliably. This project adheres to that methodology: evaluating the proposed simulation within a virtual testbed allows input modality to be manipulated independently while spatial accuracy and efficiency are recorded without interference from secondary surgical elements. Although this approach prioritises internal validity, the highly controlled environment may not replicate the full cognitive load of a live operating theatre.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Mathematically Modelling Target Acquisition}
\label{sec:fitts_law}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

To objectively measure interaction efficiency across different modalities, actions like target acquisition must follow a mathematical model. Fitts' Law dictates that movement time $MT$ scales linearly with the Index of Difficulty $ID$ \citep{Soukoreff2004TowardsHCI, MacKenzie1992FittsReader}:
\begin{equation}
    MT = a + b \cdot ID, \quad \text{where} \quad
    ID = \log_2\!\left(\frac{2D}{W}\right)
        \label{eq:fitts}
    \end{equation}
for target distance $D$, target width $W$ and empirically determined device constants $a$ and $b$.

A small, precise target such as the liver mesh constitutes a high-ID task; a modality that completes it quickly is said to exhibit high Throughput. Fitts' Law was originally developed for 2D pointing and its extension to 3D environments remains debated since depth perception is not captured by the standard formula. Acknowledging this limitation, Fitts' Law is used here to justify Task Completion Time (TCT) and spatial error as the primary objective metrics rather than to derive formal ID predictions.

While these mathematical frameworks provide numerical insight, they do not account for the user's cognitive experience in the virtual environment. Understanding HTR acceptance therefore requires examining the psychology of virtual embodiment.
% =============================================================================
\section{The Psychology of Virtual Embodiment}
\label{sec:psychology_embodiment}
% =============================================================================
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Early Immersion and Proprioception Models}
\label{sec:early_immersion}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Early psychological frameworks focused on presence (the subjective feeling of `being there') and proprioception (subconscious limb awareness), observing that humans can project spatial agency into virtual avatars \citep{Kilteni2012TheReality}. These models were critiqued for conflating technological immersion with psychological presence, treating embodiment as a binary state rather than a continuous, multidimensional construct. Consequently, researchers failed to diagnose and understand user responses to specific interaction metaphors; they could not explain why manipulating tissue with a physical joystick versus a bare hand elicits fundamentally different cognitive responses \citep{Rekik2019UnderstandingTask}. The proposed system therefore deploys multidimensional subjective instruments to capture the full range of user experience rather than a single satisfaction score.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Defining the Sense of Embodiment}
\label{sec:defining_soe}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\citet{Kilteni2012TheReality} formalised the Sense of Embodiment (SoE) as a multidimensional construct comprising three pillars: Sense of Self-Location (spatial presence within the volume), Sense of Agency (feeling of global motor control) and Sense of Body Ownership (feeling that the avatar is one's biological body). For example, CTR may deliver a high Sense of Agency, mimicking the holding of tools like a surgeon would a scalpel but they inherently restrict Body Ownership because the brain processes the controller as an external tool. HTR theoretically maximises Ownership through 1:1 mapping, but only where visual feedback precisely matches proprioceptive expectations. While this taxonomy is analyticallyuseful, the three components are not statistically independent: high Agency can mask low Ownership in self-report scenarios, complicating interpretation of subjective data.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The Uncanny Valley of Interaction}
\label{sec:uncanny_valley}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Attempts to maximise Body Ownership introduce their own vulnerability coined the `uncanny valley of interaction'. \citet{Argelaguet2016TheRepresentation} found that highly realistic virtual hands increase Ownership but degrade Agency and task performance when bodily anomalies occur, such as delayed response or inaccurate finger rendering. Hardware-induced anomalies from optical tracking (occlusion, latency) exacerbate this, as they violate the brain's strict expectations for biological limbs and break the illusion of reality \citep{Pardo2026AnalyzingUsers}. Conversely, simplified or abstract hand representations lower expectations, preserving Agency and reducing frustration during complex spatial tasks \citep{Argelaguet2016TheRepresentation}. The proposed system therefore prioritises Agency through a simplified virtual hand model rather than pursuing aesthetic realism. This leads to the question of how to quantify the mental effort required to maintain interaction quality --- the measurement of cognitive load.
% =============================================================================
\section{Cognitive Load Measurement in Virtual Reality}
\label{sec:vr_cog_load}
% =============================================================================

Cognitive load is the primary performance determinant in surgical simulation; a surgeon overloaded by the interaction interface cannot simultaneously reason about anatomy \citep{Mao2021ImmersiveReview}. \citet{Yu2021CognitiveSurgery} used EEG and heart rate during VR laparoscopic simulation, finding that VR training significantly improved surgical performance ($p < 0.01$) and lowered cognitive load. Conversely, \citet{Luong2019StudyingEnvironments} concluded that VR immersion itself has no intrinsic influence on mental effort; the cognitive bottleneck is almost entirely determined by the physical interaction mechanics. This discrepancy directly motivates the within-subjects comparative design, which isolates input modality as the sole independent variable.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Subjective Evaluation Instruments}
\label{sec:subjective_instruments}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Objective metrics capture what the user \emph{did} but not what the user \emph{experienced}. Subjective instruments quantify cognitive and ergonomic cost from the participant's perspective. Two instruments are deployed in this project.
\subsubsection{Raw NASA Task Load Index}
\label{sec:rtlx_instrument}

The NASA Task Load Index was developed by \citet{Hart1988DevelopmentResearch} to address the inadequacy of single workload measures for capturing the specific causes of user strain during complex aerospace tasks. It decomposes perceived workload into six subscales: Mental Demand, Physical Demand, Temporal Demand, Performance, Effort and Frustration, each rated on a 20-point scale.

This project uses the unweighted variant known as the Raw TLX (RTLX), which averages these directly. The original NTLX required a time-consuming weighting process based on pairwise comparisons. However, \citet{Hart2006Nasa-TaskLater} conducted a retrospective analysis across 550 published studies over the last 20 years, validating that this approach maintains experimental sensitivity without pairwise weighting overhead.

Recent literature comparing HTR and CTR across paramedical VR tasks confirmed its sensitivity to modality-specific workload differences \cite{Button2025XRInteractions} and that scores correlate directly with objective interaction errors \citep{Fischer2025ProbabilisticInteractions}, validating its appropriateness for the proposed simulation. A known limitation is susceptibility to social desirability bias; participants may underreport strain to appear competent \citep{Yu2021CognitiveSurgery}. This is addressed by triangulating RTLX scores against objective telemetry rather than treating either measure as independently sufficient.
\subsubsection{System Usability Scale}
\label{sec:sus_instrument}

The System Usability Scale (SUS) \citep{Brooke1995SUS:Scale} measures whether the interface itself is learnable, efficient and satisfying, providing a ten-item Likert instrument producing a composite 0--100 score.

\section{Biomechanics, Fatigue and Ergonomics}
\label{sec:biomechanics_fatigue}
% =============================================================================

Even where embodiment is well-preserved, physical sustainability must be evaluated independently. HTR requires continuous mid-air interaction (MAI) without physical resting surfaces, accelerating muscle exhaustion and increasing Repetitive Strain Injury risk \citep{Huang2021User-DefinedPreference}. Beyond muscular strain, the absence of physical weight fundamentally alters movement dynamics: \citet{Tang2024AbsenceInteraction} demonstrated that manipulating a weightless virtual object produces erratic corrective movements and forces visual fixation on the target, transferring attentional load from spatial reasoning to manual monitoring and potentially inducing optical fatigue \citep{Jonnakuti2023SeeingOphthalmologists}. The weight of the controller partially offsets this by providing the tactile grounding that supports Body Ownership. In a surgical context where prolonged precision is mandatory, optical HTR must minimise these disembodying movements to preserve embodiment and prevent accuracy degradation.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The Gorilla Arm Effect and Consumed Endurance}
\label{sec:gorilla_arm_baseline}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Sustained free-hand interaction induces the `Gorilla Arm' effect: rapid onset of heaviness and exhaustion in the upper limbs, demonstrating that tasks requiring continuous arm elevation induce measurable muscular failure and degrade interaction performance well within the duration of a typical VR session \citep{QPalmeira2023QuantifyingStudy}. \citet{Hincapie-Ramos2014ConsumedInteractions} formalised this into the Consumed Endurance (CE) metric, a non-intrusive skeletal-tracking algorithm that computes the ratio of elevated holding time to maximum endurance threshold, providing a continuous real-time index of ergonomic strain without interrupting the task.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Fatigue-induced Compensation and Tracking Reliability}
\label{sec:compensatory_variance}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

CE has a recognised limitation: it relies on population-average strength values and assumes no fatigue below 15\% of maximum voluntary contraction, underestimating cumulative strain in low-force intermittent actions such as the pinch gesture. \citet{Jang2017ModelingMotion} addressed this with a personalised Cumulative Arm Fatigue (CAF) model, demonstrating that individual shoulder torque estimates substantially improve fatigue prediction and explain the wide inter-individual variability observed under identical conditions.

Beyond subjective discomfort, fatigue degrades the physical reliability of HTR itself. \citet{Yang2019EffectsTask} demonstrated that joint-localised fatigue causes compensatory postural changes, such as increased trunk rotation, that alter hand position relative to the sensor field of view, increasing optical occlusion and tracking jitter. \citet{Fischer2025ProbabilisticInteractions} terms this the delimitation problem: fatigue-induced tremors cause hands to enter tracking blind-spots unintentionally, producing object drops that corrupt the kinematic data. Controller weight stabilises these tremors through haptic grounding \citep{Rantamaa2023ComparisonReality}. In the proposed simulation, grip variance serves as a continuous, non-intrusive proxy for these fatigue-induced micro-tremors.
% =============================================================================
\section{Hardware Realities: Controllers vs.\ Optical Tracking}
\label{sec:hardware_realities}
% =============================================================================
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Haptic Grounding and Proximity Preferences}
\label{sec:haptic_diff}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Physical controllers provide haptic feedback that optical HTR cannot replicate \citep{Rantamaa2023ComparisonReality}. \citet{Buckingham2021HandChallenges} term the consequence `VR Agnosia': without physical weight or texture, users adopt `pantomime grasping' strategies, miming rather than genuinely interacting. This fundamentally alters how the brain estimates grip force and spatial trajectory, degrading alignment precision in ways that self-report instruments alone may not capture.

Despite this, user preference does not uniformly favour controllers. \citet{Luong2023ControllersReality} found that CTR outperforms HTR for distant ray-casting tasks where an arms-down posture minimises exertion, but that users strongly prefer HTR for near-field spatial manipulation despite the lack of haptics. The proposed simulation is explicitly a near-field task, best designed for HTR, establishing it as the usability baseline hypothesis for the tasks.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Sensor Noise, Drift and Rotation Representation}
\label{sec:sensor_noise}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Physical controllers rely on Inertial Measurement Units (IMUs), which accumulate compounding positional error over time, known as spatial drift, requiring frequent recalibration in precision-critical environments \citep{Singh2025EvaluationView}. HTR avoids IMU drift by computing absolute position visually on each frame. However, it introduces optical occlusion: the first-generation LMC lost reliable tracking when fingers approached within 12\,mm of each other, producing severe jitter \citep{Mahdikhanlou2021ObjectGestures}. The newer LMC2 partially addresses this, achieving sub-millimetre accuracy under controlled static conditions \citep{Matuszczyk2025Comparative2}. However, this validation used static poses rather than the dynamic 6-DoF manipulation required by the proposed alignment task, therefore its generalisation to live interaction remains unconfirmed.

Independently of the tracking modality, computing rotation introduces a further instability. Euler angles are susceptible to Gimbal Lock: the loss of a rotational DoF when two axes align, producing erratic orientation jumps. Unit quaternions eliminate this entirely, providing a continuous, mathematical stable representation of 3D orientation \citep{Fang1998RealQuaternions}. Quaternion-based rotation is therefore treated as mandatory for both modalities in the proposed system, ensuring measured rotational error reflects genuine interaction difficulty rather than a mathematical flaw.

Existing comparative studies offer only cautious conclusions. \citet{Khundam2021ATraining} found no statistically significant difference in TCT or SUS between CTR and HTR during a VR intubation simulation, but reported standard deviations exceeding 90 seconds for task times and 25 points for SUS scores, severely limiting inferential power. However, their small sample size ($N=15$) limit the strength of this conclusion. \citet{Saran2025ComparingPerformance} reported similar near-equivalence with notable variance in subjective presence scores ($SD=11.65$ to $14.69$). The present study provides a more rigorous evaluation on current enterprise hardware, aiming to mitigate this noise and produce more convincing results.
% =============================================================================
\section{Software Compensation for Hardware Limitations}
\label{sec:software_compensation}
% =============================================================================

Hardware limitations cannot be eliminated through physical engineering alone. Software must compensate for optical occlusion, sensor drift and the Heisenberg effect before raw hardware data can drive a precision interaction system reliably.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Algorithmic Compensation and the Heisenberg Effect}
\label{sec:algorithmic_heisenberg}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Early HTR systems used predictive algorithms to smooth noise by anticipating a user's intended target from velocity and trajectory \citep{Mueller2017Real-TimeSensor}. While effective at smoothing data, these models reduce absolute user control. In surgical contexts, where vascular structures are separated by millimetres, algorithmic guessing is clinically unacceptable; deterministic, deliberate selection is mandatory.
\citet{Argelaguet2013AEnvironments} formalised the relevant taxonomy, dividing 3D selection into the Virtual Hand for near-field direct manipulation and the Virtual Pointer for far-field ray-casting. Within this framework, \citeauthor{Argelaguet2013AEnvironments} identified the Heisenberg effect: the physical act of pinching inadvertently displaces the hand, altering the target's spatial coordinates. Controllers partially dampen this through haptic resistance which is not present in bare-hand tracking. The proposed system must therefore enforce strict proximity and gesture validity constraints to reject ambiguous inputs before they reach the interaction logic.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Deterministic Finite Automata in Interactive Systems}
\label{sec:fsm_theory}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Probabilistic compensation resolves noise at the input level, but a separate problem exists at the system level. Reactive systems driven by continuous hardware input are vulnerable to undefined behaviour when an unanticipated event arrives in an unanticipated state. In safety-critical domains such as surgical simulation, this is intolerable.

Finite automata provide the formal mechanism to eliminate this ambiguity. A Deterministic Finite Automaton (DFA) is defined by the 5-tuple $M = (Q, \Sigma, \delta, q_0, F)$, where $Q$ is a finite set of states, $\Sigma$ is a finite input alphabet, $\delta : Q \times \Sigma \rightarrow Q$ is a total transition function, $q_0$ is the initial state and $F \subseteq Q$ is the set of accepting states \citep{Sipser2012IntroductionComputation}. The critical property is totality: $\delta$ must define exactly one successor state for every combination of state and input symbol. This guarantees reproducible, predictable behaviour regardless of the order or frequency of hardware events, eliminating the risk of unrecoverable states during a clinical trial.

State machines are well-established in interactive computing, governing animation, AI and interaction logic in game engines \citep{Jagdale2021FiniteDevelopment, Adegun2020DesignMachines}. However, these implementations are typically heuristic rather than formally total, leaving edge-case transitions undefined. The proposed system must handle events from two fundamentally different hardware providers (CTR and HTR) with differing, unpredictable edge-cases. Subsequently, the solution requires a formally total transition function. The specific instantiation of this DFA for the proposed interaction framework is detailed in the methodology and implementation chapters.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Multi-Modal and Hybrid Interfaces}
\label{sec:hybrid_interfaces}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The most advanced compensation strategy decouples the targeting mechanism from the manipulation mechanism entirely. \citet{Sidenmark2020OutlineReality} demonstrated that eye-tracking enables selection of fully occluded objects, bypassing hand-pointing limitations. \citet{Pfeuffer2017GazeReality} extended this with the Gaze+Pinch model: smooth pursuit eye movements resolve spatial depth while the hands execute manipulation from any comfortable position, eliminating the Gorilla Arm effect and decoupling selection from Heisenberg displacement.

However, the model has recognised limitations. Enforcing gaze confirmation before performing a hand selection is unreliable. Furthermore, any unintended glance can trigger a command, known as the `Midas Touch' problem \citep{Duchowski2017EyeEdition}, increasing error rates in precision tasks \citep{Sailer2000SpatialTasks}. Multi-modal fusion therefore requires a rigorous empirical baseline before it can be responsibly proposed. The present study establishes that baseline by characterising CTR and HTR independently.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Real-Time Metrics and Online Statistical Algorithms}
\label{sec:realtime_metrics}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

High-frequency metric accumulation in a game engine imposes a hard constraint on variance computation. Storing the full sample history triggers garbage collection at unpredictable intervals in managed-memory environments such as C\#, producing frame-time spikes manifesting as visual stuttering. The standard two-pass variance algorithm is therefore architecturally unsuitable \citep{Chan1983AlgorithmsRecommendations}.

The naïve single-pass algorithm avoids storing the data history, but it suffers from catastrophic cancellation --- a floating-point precision error that occurs when accumulated sums become large and nearly equal. This instability worsens as the trial duration increases \citep{Chan1983AlgorithmsRecommendations}.. The shifted data algorithm resolves cancellation for stationary data but degrades as the true mean drifts from its initial anchor across condition changes and task resets.

Welford's online algorithm resolves all three limitations \citep{Welford1962NoteProducts}. It maintains a running mean $\bar{x}_n$ and running sum of squared deviations $M_{2,n}$, updated per observation:
\begin{align}
    \bar{x}_n &= \bar{x}_{n-1} + \frac{x_n - \bar{x}_{n-1}}{n}
    \label{eq:welford_mean} \\[4pt]
    M_{2,n}   &= M_{2,n-1} + (x_n - \bar{x}_{n-1})(x_n - \bar{x}_n)
    \label{eq:welford_m2}
\end{align}
The unbiased sample variance is $s^2 = M_{2,n} / (n-1)$ at any point. Because each update subtracts the current mean before accumulating, running quantities remain small regardless of data magnitude, providing unconditional numerical stability. The complete state is three scalar variables, giving strictly $O(1)$ space complexity across any number of observations. The procedural form is given in Algorithm~\ref{alg:welford}.

    \section{Statistical Analysis Methods}
    \label{sec:stat_methods}
    % =============================================================================

Evaluating a within-subjects comparative study requires a rigorously justified statistical plan established prior to data collection. In small-sample pilot studies, applying parametric tests to ordinal or non-normally distributed data inflates Type~I error rates, while underpowered analyses risk obscuring genuine effects \citep{Wobbrock2011TheProcedures}.

The Shapiro-Wilk test assesses the normality of paired difference scores before any inferential comparison. It offers substantially greater statistical power than the ch test at small sample sizes \citep{MohdRazali2011PowerTests, SHAPIRO1965AnSamples}, providing an objective verdict on whether parametric or non-parametric methods are appropriate for each metric.

The Wilcoxon Signed-Rank test is the primary inferential statistic for all ordinal instruments (RTLX, SUS) and for non-normal continuous data \citep{Wobbrock2011TheProcedures}. It was deployed by \citet{Rantamaa2023ComparisonReality} on an identical Varjo and Ultraleap hardware stack, establishing it as the methodological precedent for this project. Where Shapiro-Wilk does not reject normality for continuous metrics, a paired $t$-test is reported alongside the Wilcoxon result for completeness \citep{Field2013DiscoveringRoll}, following the dual-reporting standard of \citet{Rantamaa2023ComparisonReality} and \citet{Saran2025ComparingPerformance}. Where the two diverge in significance, Wilcoxon's result is prioritised.

At pilot sample sizes, statistical significance is a function of both effect magnitude and sample size; a large, clinically meaningful effect may not reach conventional $p$-value thresholds \citep{Cohen2013StatisticalSciences}. Effect sizes therefore carry the primary evidential weight: Cohen's $d$ for parametric comparisons and rank-biserial $r$ for Wilcoxon tests. In the VR hardware literature, where comparable sample sizes frequently produce statistical equivalency \citep{Saran2025ComparingPerformance, Khundam2021ATraining}, effect sizes provide the granularity needed to characterise clinical viability and power future confirmatory trials.

The Bland-Altman method \citep{MartinBland1986STATISTICALMEASUREMENT} reveals the pattern of disagreement between modalities by plotting paired differences against their mean, characterising bias and 95\% limits of agreement. This complements inferential tests for TCT, positional error and rotational error, identifying whether disparities are systematic or concentrated at specific performance levels \citep{Mao2021ImmersiveReview}.

Pearson's $r$ and linear regression \citep{Field2013DiscoveringRoll} characterise two trend relationships: task number against TCT (learning curve across the four DoF levels) and clutch count against TCT (efficiency frontier per modality). \citet{Rantamaa2023ComparisonReality} demonstrated that clutch frequency directly predicts task duration on the same hardware stack, providing a direct methodological precedent. At small sample sizes, $r$ values are treated as descriptive indicators of trend rather than confirmatory claims.

Finally, the Root Mean Square of Successive Differences (RMSSD) \citep{DeGiorgio2010RMSSDInventory} is applied across the six RTLX subscales to quantify workload profile consistency. A high RMSSD indicates erratic, unevenly distributed cognitive load; a low RMSSD indicates uniform demand across dimensions. Consequently, if HTR yields a substantially higher RMSSD than CTR, it would indicate an ergonomically unsustainable cognitive burden for prolonged surgical tasks.

\section{Summary of Gaps and Contribution}
\label{sec:summary_gaps}
% =============================================================================

Spatial computing demonstrably relieves cognitive load in complex medical visualisation \citep{Kenngott2021IMHOTEP:Display, Pushparajah2021VirtualRepair}, but input hardware remains a bottleneck. CTR offers precise, haptic-grounded manipulation but degrades Body Ownership through its abstract mapping layer \citep{Kilteni2012TheReality, Allgaier2022ATraining}. HTR provides isomorphic 1:1 interaction \citep{Bachmann2018ReviewController} but suffers from optical occlusion, the Heisenberg effect and cumulative arm fatigue \citep{Jang2017ModelingMotion, Buckingham2021HandChallenges}.

Existing comparative studies exhibit three primary limitations. First, procedural evaluations such as VR intubation \citep{Khundam2021ATraining} do not capture the sustained, high-density 6-DoF spatial alignment required for resection planning. Second, no prior study evaluates the performance of the enterprise-grade Varjo XR-4 and Ultraleap LMC2 combination for these tasks. Third, previous subjective evaluations frequently misapply parametric tests to ordinal survey data, inflating Type~I error rates and overstating confidence in null results \citep{Wobbrock2011TheProcedures}.

This project addresses all three gaps directly. It delivers the first comparative evaluation of CTR and HTR for 6-DoF liver docking on this hardware, combining objective kinematic telemetry, per-task RTLX workload profiling and holistic SUS usability measurement within a non-parametric statistical framework.

\section{Project Aim and Objectives}
\label{sec:proj_aim_smart}
% =============================================================================

The aim of this project is to develop and evaluate a bare-hand optical tracking interaction system for full spatial manipulation of 3D anatomical models in virtual reality and to determine whether it reduces cognitive load and improves interaction quality relative to a physical VR controller baseline in the context of liver surgery planning.
% % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % %
% ENUMERATE: Research objectives
% % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % %
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Integrate HTR and CTR as interchangeable, independently testable interaction providers within a single Unity-based VR simulation, ensuring both traverse identical transformation logic and data logging pipelines.
    \item Design and implement a modular Deterministic Finite State Machine (DFSM) that translates raw hand and controller inputs into the four core manipulation actions: grab, translate, rotate and scale, in a modality-agnostic manner.
    \item Develop a telemetry pipeline that logs objective performance metrics in real time at 20\,Hz --- including TCT, positional and rotational alignment error, path inefficiency, re-engagement count and grip stability --- and exports data automatically without researcher intervention.
    \item Conduct a within-subjects pilot study comparing both modalities across four tasks of escalating DoF complexity, collecting objective kinematic data alongside standardised subjective workload and usability questionnaires.
    \item Analyse the collected data using a non-parametric statistical plan appropriate for the pilot sample size, reporting inferential test results and effect sizes to characterise practical significance and inform the design of a future confirmatory trial.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Hardware and Software Architecture}
\label{sec:hardware_arch}

The Varjo XR-4 provided mixed-reality rendering at a resolution and refresh rate suitable for clinical spatial tasks. The LMC2's sub-millimetre accuracy under controlled conditions has been independently validated \citep{Matuszczyk2025Comparative2}, providing an empirical baseline for HTR fidelity. The software architecture was designed as a decoupled, object-oriented system, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:uml_detailed}. The central design decision was a \texttt{BaseInteractionProvider} abstract class defining the mathematical contracts --- positional delta, quaternion rotation, grip strength --- consumed by the \texttt{InteractionOrchestrator}. Concrete implementations (\texttt{UltraleapInteractionProvider} and \texttt{VarjoInteractionProvider}) satisfy these contracts via modality-specific SDK calls, but the orchestrator has no knowledge of which is active. This guarantees a 1:1 comparative environment: both modalities traverse identical transformation mathematics and telemetry pipelines, ensuring any measured performance difference is attributable solely to input modality.

\subsection{Input Design and Gesture Recognition}
\label{sec:gesture_fsm_design}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Raw hardware output from both modalities is continuous and noisy. Varjo Controllers emit analog grip values that fluctuate with subtle finger pressure; the LMC2 produces a skeletal hand model subject to optical occlusion, infrared interference and the Heisenberg effect \citep{Argelaguet2013AEnvironments}. Neither stream can drive a deterministic state machine without producing erratic transitions. A gesture recognition layer was therefore designed as a validation boundary, converting raw data into confirmed interaction events before they reach the FSM. The human hand anatomy used by the LMC2 skeletal model is shown in Figures~\ref{fig:hand_dof} and~\ref{fig:hand_skeleton}.
\textbf{Spatial Anchor.} For HTR, the spatial anchor driving all translation and rotation deltas is the midpoint between thumb tip and index fingertip, interpolated at $t = 0.5$ (Figure~\ref{fig:hand_leap_enums}). This produces a stable positional vector consistent with the Virtual Hand technique identified by \citet{Argelaguet2013AEnvironments} as appropriate for near-field tasks. For CTR, the equivalent anchor is the controller's world-space transform position, stable by construction.
\textbf{Precision Pinch Signature.} A raw pinch strength threshold cannot distinguish intentional grabs from casual movements or closed fists. Following \citet{Buckingham2021HandChallenges}, a three-condition signature was defined: pinch strength must exceed a configurable threshold, the middle finger must be curled and the little finger must be curled. This finger audit rejects false positives while preserving naturalness, directly addressing VR Agnosia.
\textbf{Grip Threshold.} For CTR, analog grip values below a configurable threshold are ignored, preventing the first-grab-wins locking mechanism from activating on incidental contact. The threshold was calibrated empirically during prototype validation.
\textbf{Proximity Radius.} A grab event is only registered if the validated spatial anchor is within a configurable radius of the mesh centroid at the confirmation of gesture. This enforces the near-field Virtual Hand metaphor and prevents uninstructed distant grabs from corrupting the telemetry record.
\textbf{Coyote Time Buffer.} Single-frame occlusion events would otherwise drop the held object, increment the clutch count and corrupt the kinematic record. A configurable grace period sustains the last valid tracking state after loss is detected. If tracking restores within the window the interaction continues seamlessly; if not, the FSM transitions to CLUTCHED, preserving mesh position. This directly mitigates the delimitation problem.

    \subsection{Interaction Design and Finite State Machine}
    \label{sec:fsm_design}
    % -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

As established in Section~\ref{sec:fsm_theory}, safety-critical reactive systems must operate under a formally total transition function. The interaction framework was therefore designed as a Deterministic Finite State Machine (DFSM), ensuring every validated hardware event from either modality produces exactly one unambiguous state transition. Because both providers expose identical validated event types after the gesture recognition layer, the same transition function governs both conditions without modification.

Four states isolate the distinct kinematic phases of the docking task:
\begin{multicols}{2}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep, label=$q_{\arabic*}$, start=0]
    \item \textbf{(IDLE):} Resting state. No transformations applied.
    \item \textbf{(CLUTCHED):} Spatial lock. Mesh coordinates are frozen.
    \item \textbf{(TRANSFORMING):} Monomanual active state. Translation
    and rotation deltas applied continuously.
        \item \textbf{(SCALING):} Bimanual active state. Uniform scaling
    calculated relative to a locked spatial pivot.
    \end{enumerate}

\subsection{Trial Orchestration and Data Pipeline}
\label{sec:trial_orchestration_design}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The interaction framework governs manipulation within a single task. A separate architectural concern is sequencing tasks, swapping conditions and resetting the environment between sessions. Conflating orchestration with interaction or telemetry logic would violate the Testbed Evaluation separation of concerns \citep{Bowman2001TestbedTechniques}: task management must not contaminate the kinematic data stream. The \texttt{TrialOrchestrator} was therefore designed as a dedicated, decoupled master sequence manager governed by three decisions. First, task progression is fully deterministic with no branching logic, ensuring identical task exposure across all participants. Second, DoF constraints are applied via a byte-level bitmask, delegating enforcement to the \texttt{InteractionOrchestrator}. Third, condition swapping is triggered automatically by sequence completion, preventing accidental mid-trial modality changes. Keyboard overrides handle legitimate researcher interventions (pause, force-complete, task navigation) on a separate execution path that does not affect telemetry once a task is active.

The telemetry pipeline imposed three architectural constraints. A 20\,Hz polling rate was selected: sufficient temporal resolution for 6-DoF manipulation (movement frequencies rarely exceed 2--3\,Hz) while avoiding the $\approx$324,000 disk operations per hour that per-frame writes would produce. All rows are buffered in a \texttt{StringBuilder} and written atomically at task completion, guaranteeing every exported file is either complete or absent. The CSV schema maps directly onto the objective performance metrics
with no post-processing transformation required. File naming encodes participant ID, condition, task index, task name and timestamp for unambiguous joining across sessions. Grip variance is accumulated using Welford's online algorithm (Section~\ref{sec:realtime_metrics}) to maintain $O(1)$ space and avoid garbage collection pressure at 90\,Hz.

\section{Experimental Methodology}
\label{sec:experimental_methodology}
% =============================================================================

This study adopts a mixed-methods paradigm, triangulating objective kinematic telemetry with ordinal workload and usability instruments. As established in Section~\ref{sec:testbed_evaluation}, the Testbed Evaluation framework \citep{Bowman2001TestbedTechniques} mandates decomposing complex procedures into isolated, controlled sub-tasks so that input modality can be manipulated as the sole independent variable.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Experimental Design and Protocol}
\label{sec:experimental_design}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

This study was conducted as a pilot crossover feasibility trial in accordance with the CONSORT extension for pilot and feasibility studies \citep{Eldridge2016CONSORTTrials}. A within-subjects design was selected so each participant served as their own control, eliminating inter-individual variance in motor skill and spatial ability.

Four tasks of increasing complexity were designed following the Testbed principle of isolating DoF, detailed in Table~\ref{tbl:task_dof_descriptions}. Task completion was validated by a 3.0-second spatial dwell criterion: the liver model must continuously satisfy Euclidean positional error $\leq 0.05$\,m and angular rotational error $\leq 5.0\degree$ simultaneously for the full duration.
\begin{multicols}{2}
All participants completed CTR as Condition~1 before HTR as Condition~2. This fixed order was chosen for two reasons. First, four of the eight enrolled participants had prior VR controller experience versus only one with prior HTR experience (Table~\ref{tbl:participant_characteristics}); administering the more familiar condition first reduces the risk of VR acclimatisation confounding the HTR measurements. \\
Second, \citet{Pardo2026AnalyzingUsers} demonstrated that HTR involves a genuinely novel motor skill that does not transfer from controller experience, justifying its placement as the novel independent variable. The fixed order does introduce a potential learning effect; this is acknowledged as a design limitation of the fixed-order crossover.

\subsection{Task Constraint Application via Bitmask}
\label{sec:bitmask_impl}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The \texttt{TrialTask} enumeration encodes the degrees-of-freedom permissions for each task as a three-bit mask using the \texttt{byte} underlying type:
% % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % %
% ENUMERATE: TrialTask bitmask values
% % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % % %
\begin{enumerate}[nosep, label=\texttt{0b\,\textsubscript{\arabic*}}]
    \setcounter{enumi}{-1}
    \item[\texttt{0b0001}] \texttt{PureTranslation} --- bit 0 set; translation permitted.
    \item[\texttt{0b0010}] \texttt{PureRotation} --- bit 1 set; rotation permitted.
    \item[\texttt{0b0011}] \texttt{SixDegreesOfFreedom} --- bits 0 and 1 set; translation and rotation permitted.
    \item[\texttt{0b0111}] \texttt{FullScale} --- bits 0, 1 and 2 set; translation, rotation and scaling permitted.
\end{enumerate}

At task start, \texttt{ApplyTaskConstraints} reads the bitmask and writes the three boolean flags on the \texttt{InteractionOrchestrator} directly:
\begin{lstlisting}[language={[Sharp]C}, caption={Bitmask constraint
application in \texttt{TrialOrchestrator.ApplyTaskConstraints}.},
label={lst:bitmask}]
private void ApplyTaskConstraints()
{
        byte mask = (byte)currentTask;
    interactionOrchestrator.allowTranslation = (mask & 0b0001) != 0;
    interactionOrchestrator.allowRotation    = (mask & 0b0010) != 0;
    interactionOrchestrator.allowScaling     = (mask & 0b0100) != 0;
}
\end{lstlisting}

This single-operation constraint application is preferable to a switch-case enumeration over task values because it scales to any future task definition without modification: adding a new \texttt{TrialTask} value with the appropriate bitmask automatically produces the correct constraint combination without any changes to \texttt{ApplyTaskConstraints}. The correctness of this logic across all four task values is verified by the \texttt{TrialTaskBitmaskTests} EditMode test suite (Section~\ref{sec:impl_validation}).
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Spatial Dwell Validation}
\label{sec:dwell_impl}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The 3.0-second dwell criterion specified in Section~\ref{sec:task_design} is enforced by \texttt{ValidateSpatialDwell}, which executes on every \texttt{Update} frame while a task is active. The method evaluates three independent spatial criteria simultaneously and accumulates a timer only while all active criteria are continuously satisfied:

The positional error is computed as the Euclidean distance between the liver's local position and the target hologram's local position. The rotational error is computed via \texttt{Quaternion.Angle}, consistent with the quaternion-based representation mandated by Section~\ref{sec:sensor_noise} to prevent Gimbal Lock singularities \citep{Fang1998RealQuaternions}. The scale error is computed as the absolute ratio difference between the liver's uniform scale and the target's uniform scale.

Each criterion is evaluated against the task-specific tolerance thresholds defined in Section~\ref{sec:task_design}, and the active set of criteria is determined by the current \texttt{TrialTask} value via \texttt{EvaluateTaskCriteria}, which mirrors the bitmask logic: T1 requires only positional validity, T2 requires positional and rotational validity, T3 requires both, and T4 requires all three simultaneously. If any active criterion is violated while the dwell timer is accumulating, the timer is reset to zero without penalty, requiring the participant to re-establish and hold the aligned pose for the full duration from the beginning. This reset behaviour is verified by the \texttt{DwellTimer\_ResetsToZero\_WhenUserExitsTolerance} PlayMode integration test (Section~\ref{sec:impl_validation}).

\section{Telemetry Pipeline}
\label{sec:telemetry_impl}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Polling and Memory-Buffered Export}
\label{sec:telemetry_pipeline_impl}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The logger operates across two execution contexts within the Unity lifecycle. \texttt{FixedUpdate} runs at the physics timestep and performs two operations on every frame: continuous accumulation of path length, rotation and clutch count, and a polling check that appends a CSV row to the \texttt{StringBuilder} buffer when the elapsed poll timer exceeds the 20\,Hz interval. The separation between continuous accumulation and scheduled polling is deliberate: path inefficiency must be computed from every physics frame to avoid aliasing errors caused by large inter-frame displacements at high movement velocities, whereas the 20\,Hz sampled output is sufficient for the statistical analysis pipeline.

The \texttt{StartTrialLog} method initialises the buffer and writes the CSV header row. The header columns map directly onto the analysis variables defined in Section~\ref{sec:objective_metrics}:

The \texttt{TrackClutchCount} method implements this by comparing the current FSM state against the last recorded state on every physics frame:

private void TrackClutchCount()

                                bool isGrabbing = currentState == InteractionState.TRANSFORMING

\subsection{Welford Online Variance}
\label{sec:welford_impl}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Grip variance is accumulated on every physics frame in which the FSM is in an active grabbing state, using the three-variable Welford accumulator introduced in Section~\ref{sec:welford}:
\begin{lstlisting}[language={[Sharp]C}, caption={Welford online variance
accumulation in \texttt{SessionTelemetryLogger.UpdateWelfordVariance}.},
label={lst:welford}]
private void UpdateWelfordVariance(float currentValue)
{
        gripSampleCount++;
    float delta  = currentValue - gripMean;
    gripMean    += delta / gripSampleCount;
    float delta2 = currentValue - gripMean;
    gripM2      += delta * delta2;
}

private float GetCurrentGripVariance()
{
        if (gripSampleCount < 2) return 0f;
    return gripM2 / (gripSampleCount - 1);
}
\end{lstlisting}

The implementation maintains three scalar accumulators (\texttt{gripSampleCount}, \texttt{gripMean}, \texttt{gripM2}) in fixed heap memory throughout the task. No grip history array is allocated; the garbage collector is never invoked by the variance computation regardless of task duration, satisfying the $O(1)$ space requirement established in Section~\ref{sec:telemetry_design}. The Bessel-corrected denominator \texttt{gripSampleCount - 1} produces an unbiased sample variance rather than a population variance, consistent with the statistical treatment in Section~\ref{sec:objective_metrics}. The numerical correctness of this implementation is verified against a two-pass ground truth by the \texttt{WelfordVarianceTests} EditMode suite across datasets of uniform grip, high-variability alternating grip, and 1000-sample simulated trial data (Section~\ref{sec:impl_validation}).

\subsubsection{Prototype Validation and Architectural Motivation}
\label{sec:prototype_motivation}
% . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .
As established in Section~\ref{sec:prototype}, \citet{Sommerville2016GlobAlEdiTioN} advocates prototyping as a risk-reduction strategy for validating technical feasibility before full implementation. Prior to engineering the decoupled architecture described in Section~\ref{sec:hardware_arch}, we developed a monolithic proof-of-concept --- the \texttt{UltraleapKinematicManipulator} (Appendix~\ref{alg:kinematic_manipulator}) --- to validate that the Ultraleap LMC2 could produce stable, low-latency kinematic data suitable for 6-DoF mesh manipulation inside Unity.

The prototype confirmed hardware viability: pinch detection was stable, quaternion-based rotation prevented Gimbal Lock as required by Section~\ref{sec:sensor_noise} \citep{Fang1998RealQuaternions}, and the thumb-index midpoint vector provided a reliable spatial anchor for translation. However, three structural limitations made it unsuitable for production.
\textbf{Provider coupling} \\
Grab detection iterates directly over \texttt{frame.Hands} from the \texttt{LeapServiceProvider}, hardcoding the logic to the Ultraleap SDK. Substituting the Varjo Controller modality would require rewriting the class entirely, violating the internal validity requirement of the Testbed Evaluation framework \citep{Bowman2001TestbedTechniques}: both modalities must traverse identical transformation mathematics so that any measured performance difference is attributable solely to input modality.
\textbf{Undefined interaction states} \\
DoF constraints were exposed as serialised boolean flags toggled manually between tasks. An unanticipated input sequence could therefore leave the mesh in a kinematically undefined configuration. As established in Section~\ref{sec:dfsm_theory}, reactive systems must enforce a formally total transition function to guarantee deterministic behaviour \citep{Sipser2012IntroductionComputation}; boolean flags provide no such guarantee.
\textbf{Absent telemetry} \\ % Briefer []
Positional and rotational offsets were applied on every frame but never recorded. The prototype could confirm kinematic feasibility but could not produce the Task Completion Time, spatial error, inefficiency, clutch count or grip variance metrics defined in Section~\ref{sec:objective_metrics}, making it architecturally incompatible with the trial's data collection requirements.

These three limitations directly defined the production architecture. Provider coupling demanded an abstract \texttt{BaseInteractionProvider} contract. Undefined states demanded the formally specified DFSM introduced in Section~\ref{sec:fsm_design}. Absent telemetry demanded the \texttt{SessionTelemetryLogger} operating on Welford's online algorithm (Section~\ref{sec:welford}) to accumulate grip variance in $O(1)$ space without garbage collection pressure on the 90\,Hz render loop. The following sections detail each production component in turn.

As established in Section~\ref{sec:fsm_theory}, a Deterministic Finite Automaton guarantees total transition coverage for every input-state combination. In Section~\ref{sec:fsm_design}, we defined the four interaction states and modality-based input alphabet at the architectural level. This section details the instantiation of that model within the Unity engine, including the complete transition function, formal computation proof and hardware-specific implementation parameters.

For example, when a switch-case statement has no \texttt{default} case, the input is simply ignored. \subsubsection{Formal Definition of Computation}
\label{}
% . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .
Let $M = (Q, \Sigma, \delta, q_0, F)$. \\
Let $w = w_1w_2\dots w_n$ be a sequence of hardware inputs where $w_i \in \Sigma$. \\
$M$ accepts $w$ if and only if there is a sequence of states $r_0r_1r_2\dots r_n \in Q$ such that: \[
    \begin{matrix}
   r_0=q_0 \\
   \delta(r_i, w_{i+1}) = r_{i+1} & \text{for } 0\le i < N \\
   r_n \in F
\end{matrix}
\]

Thus, $M$ recognises the language of hardware inputs if $A=\{w|M \text{ accepts } w\}.$

\subsection{Objective Performance Metrics}
\label{sec:objective_metrics}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

All objective metrics were logged by the \texttt{SessionTelemetryLogger} at 20\,Hz via a memory-buffered pipeline and exported atomically to CSV at task completion. The following metrics were captured:
\textbf{Task Completion Time (TCT).} The elapsed time from task start to successful spatial dwell validation, measured in seconds. TCT is the primary objective performance metric, consistent with the systematic review evidence that VR-trained groups complete spatial procedures 18--43\% faster than control groups \citep{Mao2021ImmersiveReview}. In accordance with Fitts' Law \citep{Soukoreff2004TowardsHCI}, TCT is expected to scale with task Index of Difficulty, providing a mathematical basis for comparing performance across the four tasks of increasing kinematic complexity.
\textbf{Positional Error (m) and Rotational Error ($\degree$).} Final Euclidean distance between the liver centroid and the docking target, and \texttt{Quaternion.Angle} between the liver orientation and the target orientation, measured at the moment the dwell criterion is first satisfied. The use of quaternion-based angular error is mandatory, as established in Section~\ref{sec:sensor_noise}, to prevent Gimbal Lock singularities \citep{Fang1998RealQuaternions}. These metrics serve as the direct quantification of spatial accuracy referenced by the dwell thresholds ($\leq 0.05$\,m and $\leq 5.0\degree$).
\textbf{Path Inefficiency and Rotation Inefficiency.} The ratio of the actual path length (or total rotation) traversed by the liver to the theoretical minimum required to reach the docking pose. A ratio of 1.0 indicates a perfectly direct trajectory; values above 1.0 quantify wasted motion. These metrics extend the Testbed framework \citep{Bowman2001TestbedTechniques} by capturing the quality of the trajectory rather than only its endpoint.
\textbf{Clutch Count.} The number of discrete grab-release cycles executed during a task. As established by \citet{Rantamaa2023ComparisonReality} using identical Varjo and Ultraleap hardware, repeated interaction events serve as a reliable proxy for optical tracking instability and interaction frustration. A high clutch count indicates that the user was unable to maintain a continuous grip, necessitating repeated re-engagements.
\textbf{Grip Variance.} The running unbiased sample variance of raw grip strength (pinch strength for HTR; analog trigger deflection for CTR) during active manipulation frames, computed using Welford's online algorithm (Section~\ref{sec:welford}). Higher variance indicates less stable and consistent actuation, consistent with the biomechanical evidence that optical hand tracking produces greater grip variability than mechanically grounded physical controllers \citep{Allgaier2022ATraining}.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis Plan}
\label{sec:stat_plan}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\textbf{Normality testing} \\
Prior to inferential testing, the Shapiro-Wilk test will be applied to all difference scores (HTR minus CTR per metric). At $n = 5$, Shapiro-Wilk is the appropriate normality test as it has greater power than alternatives for small samples.
\textbf{Primary inferential statistics} \\
For metrics where difference scores are normally distributed, a paired sample $t$-test will be reported. For non-normally distributed metrics, and as the primary statistic given the ordinal nature of the RTLX and SUS data, the Wilcoxon Signed-Rank test will be used. Both tests will be reported alongside Cohen's $d$ (paired) and rank-biserial $r$ as effect size measures. At $n = 5$ the Wilcoxon cannot produce $p < .05$ unless the result is perfectly one-directional; effect sizes are therefore the primary evidence for practical significance \citep{Wobbrock2011TheProcedures}.
\textbf{Post-hoc power analysis} \\
A post-hoc power analysis will confirm the minimum detectable effect size at $\alpha = .05$ and $1 - \beta = .80$ for $df = 4$, quantifying the study's sensitivity and providing parameters for powering a future confirmatory trial.
\textbf{Carry-over effect check} \\
Because the condition order was fixed rather than counterbalanced, a period $\times$ treatment interaction check will be conducted to test whether any observed differences are attributable to learning or fatigue effects rather than modality. If a significant carry-over effect is detected, it will be reported as a confound and the affected metrics flagged for cautious interpretation.

Second, the \texttt{EnforceSpatialBounds} method in \texttt{InteractionOrchestrator} was partially tested through observable scale clamping assertions in EditMode, but full boundary coverage requires a running physics scene with a positioned mesh, which was deferred to manual integration testing.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{}
- Fixed order (learning effect) – acknowledged.
- Small sample size – not powered; treat as pilot.

It is acknowledged that reducing the analytic sample from $n = 8$ to
$n = 5$ materially impacts statistical power. A post-hoc power analysis
confirmed that at $n = 5$, the study achieves a minimum detectable effect
of $d \geq 1.69$ at $\alpha = .05$ and $1 - \beta = .80$ for a two-tailed
paired $t$-test ($df = 4$). All null results reported in
Chapter~\ref{chap5:results} should therefore be interpreted as inconclusive
rather than as evidence of equivalence between modalities. The observed
effect sizes are reported throughout to provide estimates suitable for
powering a future confirmatory trial.
- Optical occlusion and jitter – cite Mueller (2017), Gil‑Martín (2024).
- Lack of blinding (researcher administered tasks).

\subsection{Sensor Fusion and Multi-View Solutions}
\label{sec:sensor_fusion}

Looking beyond the current constraints of single-sensor systems, the future trajectory of clinical VR relies heavily on hybrid hardware architectures. To permanently resolve the issue of optical occlusion, researchers are actively developing dual-camera arrays and multi-view hand pose recognition datasets \cite{GilMartin2024}. Furthermore, for true enterprise-grade medical simulations, \citet{Cho2026} advocate for the implementation of an Adaptive Extended Kalman Filter (AEKF) to mathematically fuse optical camera data with physical force sensors, instantaneously correcting position drift. While the current project evaluates the baseline capabilities of isolated optical tracking against standard controllers, acknowledging these bleeding-edge sensor fusion architectures situates the research within the correct, forward-looking trajectory of surgical spatial computing
